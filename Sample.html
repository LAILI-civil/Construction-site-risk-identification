Training Set Creation from Real-World

Scenario: violations in construction are relatively rare, and even fewer are recorded and uploaded online. This makes it difficult to collect enough “unsafe operation” examples for training vision models. We therefore generate realistic violation samples based on real images using Stable Diffusion.

Why it matters

  • Data imbalance breaks generalization: if the training set is ~98% correct operation and ~2% violations, the model tends to learn a strong bias toward “normal,” and it may fail to detect rare unsafe behaviors in practice.
  • Safety models need balanced evidence: for reliable violation recognition, we want a more balanced distribution (e.g., ~50% vs ~50%) to reduce bias and improve recall.
  • Real-world realism matters: synthetic violation images must remain consistent with the site camera viewpoint, lighting, and background to be useful for training.

Method overview (Stable Diffusion)

We use Stable Diffusion technology to create realistic “violation” samples from real-world images. The core idea is text-to-image (and image editing): provide a prompt describing the desired change and generate a modified image that matches the prompt while preserving the original scene context.

A simple intuition: mask-and-replace. For example, mask the dog region in an image and prompt the model to replace it with a cat, while keeping the rest of the image consistent.

Strengths

  • Improves dataset balance: enables creating violation samples when real cases are rare, supporting a more balanced training distribution.
  • Real-scene consistency: edits can preserve the original camera viewpoint/background, making samples closer to deployment data than fully synthetic renders.
  • Targeted augmentation: generate specific violation types (PPE misuse, missing protective gear, wrong wearing style) with controllable prompts and masks.

Limitations

  • Visual artifacts / realism gaps: generated images may contain subtle inconsistencies (edges, shadows, reflections) that can introduce noise during training.
  • Prompt sensitivity: results depend heavily on prompt design and masking quality; repeated trials may be needed for stable outputs.
  • Label reliability: synthetic violations must be carefully reviewed to ensure they match the intended safety rule (otherwise labels can become ambiguous).
  • Distribution shift risk: if synthetic samples look systematically different from real violations, the model may overfit to “synthetic cues.”
