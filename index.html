Construction Classification (CLIP Model)

Goal: categorize the tasks performed by workers in a construction video (productivity analytics), e.g., concrete pouring, rebar work, scaffold work, and formwork work.

Why it matters (Productivity)

Construction sites often lack fine-grained, time-resolved records of what tasks are actually being performed. By classifying worker activities directly from video, we can automatically generate task timelines that support productivity measurement, progress tracking, and workflow optimization.

Example output

After inference, the system can produce time segments per activity:

=== Time segments per activity ===
- Concrete Pouring:
  0.03s -> 3.07s  (dur 3.03s)
- Rebar Work:
  12.40s -> 37.13s  (dur 24.73s)
- Scaffold Work:
  3.30s -> 6.57s  (dur 3.27s)
  7.73s -> 12.40s  (dur 4.67s)
- Formwork Work:
  37.13s -> 39.17s  (dur 2.03s)

Method overview (CLIP)

We use a CLIP model to match visual content with natural-language descriptions of construction activities. Given a video (sampled into frames or short clips) and a set of text prompts (activity labels), CLIP computes image–text similarity scores and assigns the most relevant activity to each segment.

Figure: CLIP aligns image embeddings and text embeddings, enabling zero-shot classification via prompts.

Strengths

  • Zero-shot capability: no additional training is required for new activity labels; you can extend classes by adding text prompts.
  • Fast iteration: prompt engineering enables quick experimentation without collecting and labeling a large dataset.
  • Flexible label space: supports open-vocabulary descriptions (e.g., “worker tying rebar”, “worker moving rebar”).
  • Good baseline: provides a strong starting point for productivity categorization before investing in task-specific models.

Limitations

  • Fine-grained action confusion: CLIP models are prone to errors when dealing with detailed action classifications, such as whether a worker is tying or moving steel bars during rebar construction.
  • Prompt sensitivity: prompts can help refine distinctions, but performance may still be limited when actions are visually similar.
  • Close similarity scores: for activities within the same category, similarity values can be very close, making the decision unstable.
